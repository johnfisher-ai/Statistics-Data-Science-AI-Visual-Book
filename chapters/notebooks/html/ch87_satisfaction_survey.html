Case Study: A Customer Satisfaction Survey 📝
Survey data is gloriously mixed: a yes/no recommend, categorical plans, and an ordinal 1-5 rating. We put a margin of error on the headline rate, test whether recommending depends on plan with chi-square, and compare satisfaction across segments with a rank-based test.

STEP 1 · THE QUESTION & THE DATA
What do the survey numbers really say?
A customer survey recorded a 1-5 satisfaction rating, whether each respondent would recommend (the top-2-box metric), their plan, and their segment. Three questions: what share would recommend (with a margin of error)? is recommending linked to plan? do new and existing customers differ in satisfaction?

Survey data mixes a yes/no (recommend), two categoricals (plan, segment), and an ordinal rating (1-5). Each question type needs its own tool, and the survey is a sample, so every headline number needs a margin of error, not a false air of precision.

STEP 2 · THREE QUESTIONS, THREE TESTS
A proportion, an association, and an ordinal comparison
Q1 a single RATE -> one-proportion estimate with a margin of error. Q2 two CATEGORICAL variables (plan x recommend) -> chi-square test of independence. Q3 an ORDINAL rating (1-5) across two groups -> Mann-Whitney U (ranks, not means, because 1-5 is ordinal).

A 1-5 rating is ordinal, the gap from 1 to 2 is not guaranteed equal to 4 to 5, so we compare ranks with Mann-Whitney rather than means with a t-test. And a chi-square needs expected counts of at least ~5 per cell, which we will check.

STEP 3 · RUN THE ANALYSIS
Margin of error, chi-square, and the rank test
We report the recommend rate with its margin of error, test plan-vs-recommend with chi-square (plus Cramer's V for strength), and compare satisfaction across segments with Mann-Whitney.

rec=d.would_recommend; n=len(rec); phat=rec.mean(); moe=1.96*np.sqrt(phat*(1-phat)/n)
print(f"Q1 recommend = {phat:.1%}  +/- {moe*100:.1f} pts  (95%); true rate likely in [{(phat-moe)*100:.1f}%, {(phat+moe)*100:.1f}%]")
ct=pd.crosstab(d.plan, d.would_recommend)
chi2,p,dof,expected=stats.chi2_contingency(ct)
V=np.sqrt(chi2/(n*(min(ct.shape)-1)))
print(f"Q2 plan x recommend: chi2={chi2:.2f}, dof={dof}, p={p:.4f}, Cramer V={V:.3f}, min expected={expected.min():.1f}")
print("   -> reject H0: recommending depends on plan" if p<0.05 else "   -> independent")

The recommend rate is 64% ± 3.5 points, so the true rate is very likely between about 61% and 68%, never report 64% as if it were exact. Recommending clearly depends on plan (chi-square p ≈ 0.001), though Cramer's V ≈ 0.14 says the link is modest: higher-tier plans recommend more, but plan is not destiny. And existing customers rate satisfaction higher than new ones (Mann-Whitney p ≈ 10⁻⁸) even though both medians are 4, the rank test detects the shift in the whole distribution that a glance at the medians would miss.

📋 STATISTICIAN’S REPORT
Read-out: a solid score, with a clear lever and a caveat
What we found. About 64% of customers would recommend us, with a margin of error of ±3.5 points (so realistically 61-68%). Willingness to recommend rises with plan tier (the association is statistically clear, p ≈ 0.001), and existing customers are more satisfied than new ones (a real difference, p ≈ 0.0000001).

What it means. The headline 64% is healthy but should always be quoted as a range, not a point. The plan link is real but modest (it explains only a small slice of who recommends), so 'upsell to Pro' is a lever, not a cure. The new-vs-existing gap points to an onboarding or early-experience opportunity: newer customers are measurably less satisfied.

What to do. Track the recommend rate with its margin of error over time (don't over-react to a 1-2 point wobble), and run a focused review of the new-customer onboarding experience.

Caveats. Surveys suffer non-response and self-selection, the people who answer may not represent everyone, so treat these as directional. And because satisfaction is a 1-5 ordinal scale, we compared ranks, not averages, on purpose.
