Probability Foundations & Rules

Chapters 30–31
In a nutshell
  • A probability is always between 0 and 1; the complement P(not A) = 1 − P(A) is often the easy way in.
  • OR adds (subtracting the overlap); AND multiplies, using the conditional P(B|A).
  • Independent means one event does not change the other's odds; mutually exclusive means they cannot both happen, a different idea.

Counting & Combinatorics

Chapter 32
In a nutshell
  • The multiplication principle counts multi-step choices by multiplying the options at each stage.
  • Permutations count arrangements where order matters; combinations count selections where it does not.
  • The deciding question is always "does order matter?", and both build on the factorial n!.

03

Conditional Probability & Bayes' Theorem

Chapters 33–34
In a nutshell
  • Conditional probability P(A|B) shrinks the world to the cases where B happened.
  • Bayes' theorem updates a prior belief into a posterior as evidence arrives: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
  • With a rare condition, even an accurate test yields mostly false positives, the base rate dominates.

04

Random Variables & Expectation

Chapter 35
In a nutshell
  • A random variable maps each outcome to a number; its PMF lists the probability of each value.
  • Expectation E[X] = ∑ x·P(x) is the probability-weighted average, the PMF's balance point.
  • Variance E[X²] − μ² measures spread; expectation is linear even for dependent variables.

05

The Discrete Distribution Gallery

Chapter 37
In a nutshell
  • Discrete distributions count things: successes (Binomial), waits (Geometric / Neg. Binomial), rare events (Poisson).
  • Use the Hypergeometric when you draw without replacement, so trials are not independent.
  • Pick by the question: how many, how long until, with or without replacement, and how rare.

06

Continuous Distributions & the Normal

Chapters 38–39
In a nutshell
  • For continuous variables, probability is area under the density curve, never the height at a single point.
  • The exponential models waiting time (memoryless); the gamma sums several such waits; the normal is the universal bell.
  • The empirical rule (68 / 95 / 99.7%) and the z-score turn any normal value into a standardized distance.

07

Sampling Distributions & the Central Limit Theorem

Chapter 40
In a nutshell
  • The Central Limit Theorem: the distribution of the sample mean approaches normal as n grows, whatever the population's shape.
  • The mean's spread is the standard error SE = σ/√n, which shrinks as the sample grows.
  • Because precision scales with √n, you must quadruple the sample to halve the error, the engine behind every confidence interval.
