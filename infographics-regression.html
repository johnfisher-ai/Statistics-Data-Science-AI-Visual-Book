01

Covariance: Do Two Variables Move Together?

Chapter 89
In a nutshell
  • Covariance averages the product (x − x̄)(y − ȳ): positive when the two tend to rise and fall together, negative when one rises as the other falls.
  • Its sign is meaningful, but its magnitude is not, it changes with the units (dollars vs thousands of dollars), so you cannot compare covariances across variable pairs.
  • Dividing by the two standard deviations removes the units and gives the correlation r, a comparable score bounded between −1 and +1.

02

Correlation Coefficients

Chapter 90
In a nutshell
  • Pearson's r scores the strength and direction of a straight-line relationship from −1 (perfect down) through 0 (none) to +1 (perfect up).
  • r² is the share of one variable's variance the other explains (r = 0.7 means about 49%); a moderate r explains less than intuition suggests.
  • Spearman's rank correlation is Pearson's r computed on ranks, so it captures any monotonic trend and resists outliers; remember r near 0 rules out a line, not a curve.

03

Correlation vs. Causation

Chapter 91
In a nutshell
  • A correlation between X and Y is consistent with X→Y, Y→X, a confounder Z driving both, or pure coincidence, the data alone cannot tell them apart.
  • A confounder (like hot weather behind ice cream and drownings) creates a spurious association; controlling for it can make the link shrink to nothing.
  • Causation is earned by a randomized experiment, or by careful design that blocks confounders and offers a plausible mechanism, never by correlation strength alone.

04

The Regression Line & Least Squares

Chapters 92–93
In a nutshell
  • Simple linear regression fits ŷ = b₀ + b₁x, the one line whose total squared residuals (vertical gaps between points and line) is smallest.
  • The slope b₁ is the predicted change in y for a one-unit rise in x; the intercept b₀ is the prediction at x = 0 (often just an anchor, not meaningful).
  • R² reports the fraction of y's variation the line captures; pair every prediction with a residual check and an interval, since a line can fit well on average yet miss badly in places.

05

Multiple Regression & Reading Coefficients

Chapter 94
In a nutshell
  • Multiple regression estimates each predictor's effect holding the others constant, so a coefficient answers “what does this variable add beyond the rest?”
  • Categories enter as dummy (0/1) variables and interactions let one variable's effect depend on another; use adjusted R² so extra predictors must earn their place.
  • Multicollinearity (redundant predictors, like weight and BMI) inflates coefficient variance, flagged by a high VIF (rule of thumb > 10); drop, combine, or regularize.

06

Checking the Conditions

Chapter 95
In a nutshell
  • Ordinary regression assumes Linearity, Independence, Normal errors, and Equal variance; the residual-vs-fitted plot checks most of them at a glance.
  • A flat, patternless band around zero is healthy; a funnel signals heteroscedasticity and a curve signals a missing nonlinear term.
  • Remedies are cheap: log-transform a skewed variable, add a squared or interaction term, switch to robust standard errors, and inspect high-leverage points before trusting the coefficients.

07

Beyond the Straight Line: the Model Family

Chapters 96–99
In a nutshell
  • The straight line generalizes: keep the linear predictor b₀+b₁x…, then add a link function and error distribution to fit the outcome, that is the generalized linear model (GLM).
  • Logistic regression models a yes/no outcome as a probability (coefficients become odds ratios); Poisson / negative binomial models counts with a log link.
  • Regularization (ridge, lasso, elastic net) shrinks and even zeros coefficients to fight overfitting and multicollinearity; econometrics and panel data extend regression to repeated observations over time.

08

The Regression Case-Study Workflow

Chapters 100–103
In a nutshell
  • A real regression project follows one repeatable 12-step method: define, collect, inspect, clean, visualize, transform, analyze, build, validate, interpret, deploy, and communicate.
  • The outcome type chooses the model: a number → linear (house prices), yes/no → logistic (churn), a count → Poisson / negative binomial (bike demand), many predictors → regularized (medical charges).
  • Whatever the model, explore before fitting, validate the conditions, report an interval not just a point, and finish with a plain-English write-up a non-specialist can act on.
