01

The Four Components

Chapter 127
In a nutshell
  • A time series is data indexed by time, and the order matters, each value leans on the ones before it, so you cannot shuffle it.
  • Most series decompose into trend (long-run direction), seasonality (a fixed-period repeat), a cycle (longer, variable-length swings), and an irregular remainder.
  • A centered moving average smooths away the seasonal wave to reveal the trend, the intuition behind formal decomposition.

Additive vs Multiplicative

Chapter 127
In a nutshell
  • Additive: the seasonal swing is a roughly constant amount (always about the same number of units above trend).
  • Multiplicative: the swing grows with the level (always about the same percentage), so the wave gets taller as the trend climbs.
  • A neat trick: taking logs turns a multiplicative series into an additive one, because log(T × S) = log T + log S. One line, seasonal_decompose(...), splits any series into its parts.

Stationarity, Differencing & Unit-Root Tests

Chapters 127, 129
In a nutshell
  • A series is stationary when its mean and variance do not change over time, the assumption behind most forecasting models.
  • Differencing (each value minus the previous) removes a trend and is the “I” in ARIMA; the ADF test checks it (small p = stationary), and KPSS flips the null so pair them.
  • White noise, zero mean, constant variance, no autocorrelation, is the part you cannot forecast and the goal for a model's residuals.

Reading ACF & PACF

Chapter 129
In a nutshell
  • The ACF (autocorrelation) and PACF (partial) are the classic order-picking tools: an AR(p) cuts off in the PACF, an MA(q) cuts off in the ACF.
  • A spike at the seasonal lag (12 for monthly) is the fingerprint of seasonality; a slowly-decaying ACF signals a trend to difference away.
  • A series can be white noise in its level (flat returns ACF) while its squared values are autocorrelated, the signature of volatility clustering.

05

The Forecasting Toolkit

Chapter 128
In a nutshell
  • Climb the ladder: a baseline to beat, then moving average / SES (level only), then Holt-Winters (adds trend and season), then ARIMA/SARIMA (models the autocorrelations).
  • SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)s combines AR (values) + I (differencing) + MA (errors), with a seasonal copy at lag s; it also returns a prediction interval.
  • There is no universal best model, fit several and let a held-out test decide, and always clear the seasonal-naive benchmark.

06

Backtesting: Static vs Dynamic

Chapter 128
In a nutshell
  • Never shuffle a time series: fit on the earlier part and test on the most recent part, so the evaluation mimics real forecasting.
  • A static (one-step-ahead) forecast uses the actual past value at each step and looks great; a dynamic (multi-step) forecast feeds its own predictions forward, and its error grows with the horizon.
  • Report the horizon that matches your decision, judging a one-step forecast and deploying it for year-ahead planning over-promises.

07

Volatility, GARCH & Relationships

Chapter 129
In a nutshell
  • When big moves cluster into calm and turbulent stretches, model the variance with ARCH/GARCH; a persistence (alpha + beta) near 1 means volatility shocks last.
  • GARCH forecasts the size of future moves, not their direction, the basis of Value-at-Risk and other risk measures.
  • Granger causality tests whether one series' past improves another's forecast (predictive precedence, not proof of cause); ARDL then quantifies the relationship.

08

Forecast Accuracy

Chapter 130
In a nutshell
  • Forecast error is actual − forecast; ME catches bias, MAE the typical miss, and RMSE (always at least MAE) punishes big misses most.
  • MAPE/sMAPE are scale-free but fragile near zero; MASE and Theil's U score you against the naive forecast, below 1 is the bar every model must clear.
  • A metric is a loss function, so pick the one that matches your costs, and use the Diebold-Mariano test to check whether a ranking gap is real or just luck.
