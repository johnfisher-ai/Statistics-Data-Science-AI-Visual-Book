02

Data Types & Levels of Measurement

Chapters 4–5
In a nutshell
  • Qualitative data labels things (nominal, ordinal); quantitative data measures them.
  • The four levels nominal → ordinal → interval → ratio each unlock more: order, equal gaps, a true zero.
  • The level decides which statistics and charts are valid, you cannot average a nominal category.

03

Population vs Sample

Chapter 6
In a nutshell
  • A population is everyone you care about; a sample is the subset you actually measure.
  • Parameters (μ, σ, p) describe the population; statistics (x̄, s, p̂) describe the sample.
  • The whole game of inference is using a sample statistic to estimate an unknown population parameter.

04

Describing Data: Center · Spread · Shape

Chapters 8–11
In a nutshell
  • Summarise any variable with three questions: where is its center, how wide is its spread, what is its shape.
  • Prefer the median and IQR when outliers or skew are present, they resist extremes; the mean and SD do not.
  • Comparing mean vs median instantly tells you the direction of skew.

05

Z-scores & the Empirical Rule

Chapter 12
In a nutshell
  • For a roughly normal variable, about 68% / 95% / 99.7% of values fall within ±1 / ±2 / ±3 SD of the mean.
  • A z-score rescales any value to "how many SDs from the mean", positive is above, negative below.
  • Z-scores let you compare across different units and flag outliers (often |z| > 3).

06

Choosing the Right Chart

Chapters 14–17
In a nutshell
  • Pick the chart from the job: compare → bar, distribution → histogram/box, relationship → scatter, parts → pie/stacked, over time → line.
  • Match the chart to the data type: bars for categories, histograms for numeric values.
  • When in doubt, choose the simplest chart that makes the point without a manual.

07

The Data-Cleaning Toolbox

Chapters 18–24
In a nutshell
  • Prep follows a toolbox: dedup → missing → outliers → transform → reshape → encode/scale, applied as the data needs.
  • For missing data, the reason picks the fix; for the target variable, drop rows rather than invent values.
  • Encode by type: ordinal → 1,2,3 (order kept), nominal → one-hot (no false order).
→ Review Chapters 18–24
08

The EDA Loop & Correlation

Chapters 25–29
In a nutshell
  • EDA is a loop: audit → describe → visualize → prepare, repeating until the data tells a trustworthy story.
  • Correlation runs −1 to +1: Pearson measures straight-line strength, Spearman measures any monotonic rise.
  • Two permanent warnings: correlation is not causation, and always plot the data.
